Objection 3 “Over Half of Homicide Exonerations Are Black” ▼

No. Of the exonerations, half are Black. That does not mean half of the homicides Black Americans commit end up exonerated — it means that the exonerations themselves (which total only about 35 per year nationwide) are split roughly down the middle, with about 19 Black exonerations per year and about 11 White, per the National Registry of Exonerations.

The statistic is about the tiny population of overturned convictions, not about the much larger population of committed homicides. The reason the exonerations are about half Black is the same reason the convictions are about half Black: Black Americans are the identified offender in roughly half of known-race homicides nationally.

It isn’t that half of Black people are exonerated. It’s that half of the exonerations are of Black defendants — which turns out to be a tiny fraction (0.30%) of total Black homicide offenders. The equivalent White rate is 0.23%. The gap between them is 0.07 percentage points.

Stress-Test Against California

Suppose every single one of the ~19 Black wrongful convictions per year in the entire United States had actually happened in California. Subtract 19 from the Black homicide arrest count every year, then recompute the disproportionality multiplier. The rate doesn’t move.

California Black Homicide Disproportionality — Original vs. Exoneration-Adjusted
For each year 2020–2024: arrest share ÷ 6.0% population share. “Adjusted” subtracts 19 wrongful convictions per year (the entire U.S. annual total) from the California Black count and from the California total.
Even under an impossibly generous assumption — all U.S. Black wrongful convictions happening in California — the disproportionality multiplier stays above 4.4× every year. Removing wrongful convictions slightly increases the rate, because the total homicide count shrinks too.

Objection 6 “This Is the Legacy of American Slavery” ▼

Falsifiable claim. If American slavery and legal discrimination are the cause, then (a) groups with more severe legal discrimination should show higher rates, and (b) populations with no U.S. slavery history should show different patterns, and (c) the state with the deepest slavery connection should show the highest disparity.

None of these predictions hold.

In California specifically, Chinese Americans faced legal restrictions more severe than Black Americans on every major dimension of civil rights. If historical discrimination drives present-day offending, this is where the strongest effect should appear.

About This Data
Methodology
City-level data — Perpetrator-level counts compiled from Public Records Requests to Sacramento PD, San Jose PD, Oakland PD, and San Francisco PD. All individuals identified by each department as a suspect, arrestee, or cited party in connection with the offense are included — role label varies by agency (SUSPECT, ARREST/CITE, JUV-SUSPECT, ARRESTEE, etc.) but the underlying category is "person the department identified as having committed the crime." Ethnicity takes precedence over race in bucketing to avoid Hispanic/White conflation. Mixed-victim incidents use fractional split (1/n per victim race) so no perpetrator contributes more than 1.0 to the details matrix; all cells floor-rounded. Unknown-race perpetrators are disclosed separately rather than bucketed into Other. Oakland and SF homicide data include a large share of incidents with no identified suspect (58% and 31% respectively), reflecting department clearance rates; those cases are counted in totalIncidents but excluded from race-specific breakdowns.

Statewide data comes from the California Attorney General's annual "Crime in California" reports, specifically Table 31 (Felony Arrests). These are published by the CA Department of Justice's OpenJustice portal.

Population data is sourced from U.S. Census Bureau estimates. Statewide demographics shifted significantly over the 24-year period (White: ~47% → ~34%, Hispanic: ~32% → ~40%, Black: ~6.5% → ~5.4%).

Per capita rates are calculated by dividing each group's share of suspects (city-level) or arrests (statewide) by their share of the population. 1.0x = proportional; above = overrepresented; below = underrepresented.

All source data — including the actual public records request documents, police department response files, and links to California Attorney General reports — are linked throughout this site. Nothing is hidden.
Limitations
City selection: Public records requests were submitted to California's 10 most populous cities. Response timelines and cooperation varied significantly — some departments responded within weeks, others took months of follow-up, and several denied requests or never responded. The cities represented here are those that provided usable data, not a curated sample.

Date ranges: Data periods are not uniform across cities. Some departments allow requests spanning many years, while others limit the scope to avoid overloading the caseworker assigned to process the request. As a result, Sacramento's data spans 2016–2024, while San José and San Francisco cover shorter windows (2020–2025 and 2020–2024, respectively), and Oakland covers 2020–2024. Direct city-to-city comparisons should account for these differences.

Racial categories: Race categories are reported as recorded by each police department and then unified across agencies into Black, Hispanic, White, and Other. "Other" collapses Asian (all sub-groups), Pacific Islander / Native Hawaiian, American Indian / Alaskan Native, Middle Eastern, and any catch-all "Other" values into a single bucket. Statewide population baselines for the "Other" bucket in San Jose, San Francisco, and Oakland currently use the Asian-share figure as a proxy; this slightly understates the denominator and inflates the "Other" overrepresentation ratio. Ethnicity takes precedence over race when both are recorded.

Why not FBI data? The FBI's Uniform Crime Report and Crime Data Explorer do not separate Hispanic from White. Every Hispanic arrest is counted as "White." This makes FBI city-level data unreliable for any race-based analysis in states with large Hispanic populations. California's own Attorney General data does separate Hispanic from White, which is why this investigation uses it. The distortion is not small: in 2024, California recorded 212 White homicide arrests and 629 Hispanic homicide arrests. Under FBI classification, those 629 Hispanic arrests would be added to the White total, reporting 841 "White" homicide arrests — nearly 4× the actual number. That is why this project filed individual public records requests with each department rather than relying on federal datasets.

2001 data excluded: The year 2001 is excluded from the statewide dataset. The California Attorney General's office published the same data for both the 2001 and 2002 reports — the 2001 "Crime in California" report contains identical figures to 2002, meaning the actual 2001 arrest data was never published. Because no valid data exists for that year, 2001 has been omitted. The statewide analysis covers 24 years: 2000, then 2002–2024.
